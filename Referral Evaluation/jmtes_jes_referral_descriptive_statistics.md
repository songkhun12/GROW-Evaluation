Because the updated files do not include a student-specific `Report School Days` field, referral outcomes are reported on the 170-day school-year scale used in the earlier analysis; with a common 170-day exposure, these values are equivalent to the recorded referral counts per student.

Descriptively, JMTES students had lower school-based referral frequency than JES students in the updated data. The mean total school referral frequency was 0.252 referrals at JMTES compared with 0.692 at JES, a difference-in-mean of -0.441 referrals. The median total school referral frequency was 0.000 for both schools, indicating that the typical student in both groups had no school-based referrals. The difference between the mean and median shows that referral outcomes remain right-skewed, with a smaller number of students receiving multiple referrals and pulling the mean above the median, especially at JES.

The school-level breakdown shows lower mean referral frequency at JMTES for Level I, Level II, and Level III referrals. Level IV referrals were rare at both schools, with a small positive difference-in-mean for JMTES because the mean was 0.014 at JMTES and 0.007 at JES. Across all school referral levels, the medians were 0.000 for both schools, reinforcing that most students had no referrals in each category and that the mean differences are driven by differences among students who received at least one referral.

Bus referral frequency was also lower at JMTES in the descriptive comparison. The mean total bus referral frequency was 0.026 referrals at JMTES compared with 0.163 at JES, a difference-in-mean of -0.136 referrals. The median total bus referral frequency was 0.000 for both schools, again showing that most students had no bus referrals. The gap between the mean and median is larger for JES, suggesting that a small number of JES students had higher bus referral frequency.

For bus referral levels, JMTES had lower mean Level II referral frequency than JES and very low Level I and Level III bus referral frequencies in both schools. Bus Level I events occurred only at JMTES and Bus Level III events were extremely rare in both schools, so these level-specific categories should be interpreted cautiously.

These referral descriptive statistics are unadjusted and should not be interpreted as causal estimates. They summarize school differences before applying regression adjustment.
